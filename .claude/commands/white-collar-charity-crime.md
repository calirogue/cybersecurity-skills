## 2. OPERATIONAL MECHANICS & WHITE-COLLAR SCHEMES

### How the Scheme Operates Step by Step

Map the full mechanical execution of the fraud. Distinguish clearly between:

**A. Skimming (theft from incoming revenue — hardest to detect)**
- Cash donations collected at events never deposited — treasurer pockets before entry into books
- Online donation platform credentials controlled exclusively by treasurer; deposits redirected to personal account
- Check donations endorsed and deposited to personal account before recording
- Registration fees, dues, or rent collected in cash with no receipts issued
- Detection challenge: no paper trail exists because the asset never entered the books

**B. Larceny / Misappropriation (theft from accrued assets — easier to trace, larger scale)**
- Unauthorized checks written to self, family members, or shell vendors
- Debit card linked to organizational account used for personal expenses (fuel, groceries, travel)
- ACH transfers from reserve or endowment accounts to personal bank account
- Overpayment of invoices to colluding vendors with kickback returned to perpetrator
- Fabricated invoices from non-existent vendors (ghost vendors) approved by sole signatory
- Payroll fraud: ghost employees on volunteer organization payroll, or inflated hours for real employees

**C. Disaster/Charity Solicitation Fraud (fake or look-alike organizations)**
- Registration of organization name nearly identical to legitimate charity (e.g., "American Red Crss Relief Fund")
- Launch of look-alike donation websites during high-profile disaster news cycles
- Social media fundraisers on GoFundMe, Facebook, or Instagram with fabricated beneficiary stories
- Use of stock photography to fabricate "beneficiary" imagery
- Funds collected, site taken down within 30–90 days, perpetrator disappears
- 990-N (e-Postcard) filed with IRS or no filing at all; no state registration

**D. HOA-Specific Reserve Fund Schemes**
- Reserve fund held in separate account; perpetrator obtains sole signatory access
- Funds transferred to operating account under guise of "emergency repair"
- Vendor bid-rigging: legitimate vendor quotes inflated at perpetrator's direction; kickback paid back in cash or gift cards
- No competitive bidding required by bylaws — perpetrator awards all contracts to one vendor
- Capital improvement invoices fabricated; work never performed
- Reserve study suppressed or never commissioned to hide fund depletion

**E. Youth Sports / Booster Club / PTA Schemes**
- Tournament registration fees collected in cash, not deposited
- Equipment purchases made for personal use (garage full of balls, uniforms sold privately)
- Sponsorship funds from local businesses deposited to personal account
- Travel fund advanced to treasurer, receipts never submitted, overage kept
- Dual accounting books maintained — one for members, one reflecting actual balances

### Skimming vs. Reserve Raiding — Comparison

| Dimension | Skimming | Reserve/Asset Misappropriation |
|---|---|---|
| When theft occurs | Before funds enter books | After funds are recorded |
| Detection difficulty | Very high — no paper trail | Moderate — transactions exist |
| Typical duration | Years (chronic, low-amount) | Shorter bursts (high-amount) |
| Common forensic signal | Revenue lower than expected | Unexplained account withdrawals |
| Primary statute triggered | State theft/larceny | Wire fraud, bank fraud, embezzlement |

## 3. STATUTORY FRAMEWORK & CHARGES

### Federal Jurisdiction

For each applicable federal statute, provide the citation, required proof elements, and maximum penalties:

**18 U.S.C. § 1341 — Mail Fraud**
- Elements: (1) scheme to defraud; (2) use of U.S. mail or private interstate carrier; (3) knowing and intentional participation
- Penalty: up to 20 years; up to 30 years if financial institution or federally declared disaster involved
- Application: solicitation letters, mailed financial statements, mailed checks to ghost vendors

**18 U.S.C. § 1343 — Wire Fraud**
- Elements: (1) scheme to defraud; (2) use of wire, radio, or television communication in interstate commerce; (3) intent to defraud
- Penalty: up to 20 years (30 years if disaster-related)
- Application: online donation platforms, ACH transfers, email solicitations, Zelle/Venmo transfers from org accounts

**18 U.S.C. § 666 — Theft from Organizations Receiving Federal Funds**
- Elements: (1) agent of an organization; (2) organization receives federal benefits exceeding $10,000/year; (3) theft/embezzlement of $5,000 or more
- Penalty: up to 10 years imprisonment
- Application: HOAs in federally subsidized housing, nonprofits receiving federal grants (Title I schools, HUD-affiliated associations)

**26 U.S.C. § 7201 — Tax Evasion (Individual)**
- Elements: (1) affirmative act of evasion; (2) substantial tax deficiency; (3) willfulness
- Penalty: up to 5 years, fines up to $250,000
- Application: embezzled funds constitute taxable income; perpetrator fails to report on personal return

**26 U.S.C. § 7206 — Filing False Tax Returns / Aiding**
- Elements: (1) willful making of materially false statement under penalties of perjury
- Penalty: up to 3 years per count
- Application: nonprofit treasurer who signs false Form 990 understating revenue or overstating expenses

**IRS Form 990 Fraud — Nonprofit Tax Reporting**
- 501(c)(3), 501(c)(4), and 501(c)(6) organizations must file annual Form 990/990-EZ/990-N
- Fraudulent 990 filings: understated gross receipts, false program service accomplishment narratives, false Schedule B (donor disclosure)
- IRS Criminal Investigation (IRS-CI) has jurisdiction to prosecute false 990 filings
- Organizations under $50,000 gross receipts file 990-N (e-Postcard) — minimal oversight, high fraud risk

**Corporate Transparency Act (CTA) / FinCEN Beneficial Ownership Reporting**
- Effective January 1, 2024: shell companies and certain HOA management entities must report beneficial owners to FinCEN
- Failure to report: civil penalties up to $500/day; criminal penalties up to $10,000 and 2 years imprisonment
- Application: perpetrators who funnel embezzled funds through shell LLCs (e.g., sham "maintenance companies") must now disclose true ownership

**18 U.S.C. § 1956 — Money Laundering**
- Elements: (1) financial transaction; (2) proceeds of specified unlawful activity (mail/wire fraud qualify); (3) intent to conceal or promote
- Penalty: up to 20 years
- Application: depositing embezzled funds into personal investment accounts, purchasing real estate, converting to cryptocurrency

**18 U.S.C. §§ 1961–1968 — RICO**
- Elements: (1) enterprise; (2) pattern of racketeering activity (2+ predicate acts within 10 years); (3) nexus to enterprise
- Application: rare but applicable where multiple perpetrators operate sustained embezzlement ring across multiple organizations (e.g., professional HOA management company defrauding multiple associations)

### State-Level Enforcement

**State Attorney General — Charitable Bureau / Charities Division**
- All 50 states have an AG office with jurisdiction over charitable solicitation fraud
- Powers: investigative subpoenas (no probable cause required in many states), asset freeze orders, cy pres reallocation of misused charitable funds, license revocation
- Key states with aggressive enforcement: New York (AG Charities Bureau under EPTL § 8-1.4), California (AG Registry of Charitable Trusts under Gov't Code § 12580), Illinois (Charitable Trust Act), Florida (Solicitation of Contributions Act § 496)
- **New York Specific:** EPTL § 11-2.3 (prudent investor standard for fiduciaries); Martin Act (GBL § 352) can apply if securities-adjacent misrepresentation; AG can bring derivative suit on behalf of charitable beneficiaries
- **California Specific:** Gov't Code § 12591.1 — mandatory registration before soliciting; AG can seek disgorgement, civil penalties up to $50,000 per violation, and restitution

**State Charitable Solicitation Registration Laws**
- Most states require charities soliciting funds to register annually with AG office
- Fake disaster charities routinely fail to register — triggers immediate enforcement jurisdiction
- Penalties: injunctions, fines, disgorgement of all collected funds, criminal referral

**HOA-Specific State Law**
- **Florida:** Florida Homeowners' Association Act (§ 720.303) — mandates financial reporting, reserves, independent CPA audits for associations over $500K revenue; § 720.3033 — officer liability for failure to maintain adequate records; criminal theft under FL § 812.014
- **California:** Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act (Civil Code § 5500 et seq.) — requires reserve funding plan, annual budget, independent financial review; Civil Code § 5551 (reserve fund cannot be used for non-capital expenses without member vote)
- **Texas:** Texas Property Code § 209 — HOA financial transparency requirements; audit requirements for associations over certain revenue thresholds
- **General State Embezzlement Statutes:** Grand larceny / felony theft thresholds vary by state ($1,000–$10,000 typically elevates to felony); Commercial Embezzlement statutes specifically cover fiduciary relationship (NY Penal Law § 155.05(2)(a))

**State-Level Nonprofit Corporation Acts**
- Most states have enacted nonprofit corporation acts modeled on the Revised Model Nonprofit Corporation Act (RMNCA)
- Board members owe fiduciary duties of care, loyalty, and obedience — breach creates personal civil liability
- Directors can be removed and barred from serving on any nonprofit board by court order

## 4. INVESTIGATIVE DETECTION & RED FLAGS

### Financial Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It May Indicate |
|---|---|
| Treasurer resists independent audit or delays engagement of CPA | Active concealment of fraudulent entries |
| Bank statements not shared with full board or available only in summary form | Perpetrator controlling information flow |
| Single signatory on all accounts with no dual-authorization requirement | No oversight of disbursements |
| Checks made payable to cash, treasurer personally, or vague payees ("supplies") | Direct misappropriation |
| Vendor invoices that lack address, phone number, or EIN | Ghost vendor scheme |
| Revenue significantly lower than prior years with no programmatic explanation | Skimming of cash receipts |
| Reserve fund balance declining without documented capital projects | Unauthorized reserve raids |
| Multiple small transactions just below approval thresholds | Structuring to avoid authorization requirements |
| ACH or wire transfers to unrecognized accounts | Unauthorized electronic fund transfers |
| Treasurer serves as exclusive contact for all vendors and banks | Information monopoly enabling concealment |
| Petty cash fund with no receipts or reconciliation | Chronic low-level skimming |
| Significant "miscellaneous" or "other" expense line items | Catch-all categories masking personal expenses |
| Missing or incomplete bank reconciliations | Failure to reconcile is often cover for manipulated books |
| Expense reimbursements without receipts submitted months late | Fabricated or inflated reimbursements |
| Unusual related-party transactions (payments to treasurer's family business) | Self-dealing / breach of fiduciary duty |

### Behavioral Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It May Indicate |
|---|---|
| Treasurer becomes defensive or hostile when asked financial questions | Guilty knowledge; attempts to suppress scrutiny |
| Financial reports produced only verbally, never in writing | Avoids creating documentary record |
| Treasurer insists on being present at all financial discussions | Controls narrative; blocks independent analysis |
| Sudden lifestyle upgrade (new vehicle, renovations, vacations) unexplained by salary | Visible enrichment from stolen funds |
| Unusually long tenure in financial role with resistance to transition | Entrenchment; fear of successor discovering irregularities |
| Discourages succession planning or training of backup treasurer | Maintains information monopoly |
| Delays responding to financial inquiries from board members | Buys time to fabricate explanations |
| Pushes to keep financial oversight "within the family" | Resists external scrutiny |
